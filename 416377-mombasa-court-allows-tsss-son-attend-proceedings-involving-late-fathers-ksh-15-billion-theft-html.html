Mombasa Court Allows TSS's Son to Attend Proceedings Involving Late Father's KSh 1.5 Billion Theft

Publish date: 2024-08-19
  • The family sued James Mwangi, Aweys Ahmed Mohamed, Zein Ahmed Mohamed, Zahir Abbas Khaku, and Victor Arara Were for fraud
  • Magistrate Francis Kyambia said Ahmed Sabir Shekh Said was the one who led to the charging of the accused persons before the court of law
  • The accused persons had asked the court to bar Ahmed from attending proceedings since he was listed down as one of the witnesses

A son of the late Mombasa tycoon Tahir Sheikh Said, Ahmed Sabir Shekh Said, got a reprieve after a Mombasa court allowed him to attend court proceedings against five people accused of defrauding his father’s company.

In his seven-page ruling, Mombasa chief magistrate Francis Kyambia said that it was a matter of judicial notice from the ongoing proceedings that the accused defrauded Said's father’s company by the five people.

The magistrate added the family sued James Mwangi, Aweys Ahmed Mohamed, Zein Ahmed Mohamed, Zahir Abbas Khaku, and Victor Arara Were for a crime involving companies in which they had an interest.

“The deceased’s son Ahmed Sabir Shekh Said who had testified against the accused persons is a victim,” he pointed out.

Kyambia pointed out in his ruling that the evidence adduced showed the son of the late tycoon is one of the complainants to the police who did the investigation.

The Mombasa chief magistrate added in his ruling that the deceased’s son is the one who led to the charging of the accused persons before the court of law.

The accused persons had asked the court to bar Ahmed from attending proceedings since he was listed as one of the witnesses.

However, in his defence, Ahmed informed the court that being one of the sons of the Mombasa tycoon and owner of the property, he had a right to attend court and follow proceedings like any other family member.

Kyambia pointed out that this is to the extent that the witness is classified as a victim as provided under the section of the victim’s protection Act 2014.

The five are charged with conspiring to defraud TSS by filing false share transfer and change of directorship of TSS Group of Companies with the registrar of companies by pretending that the late TSS had voluntarily released 10 per cent of shares to his wife, Isha Said.

They committed the fraud on diverse dates between February 8, 2011, and October 29, 2015, at TSS Grain Millers in Mombasa.

They are also accused of forging deeds of guarantee and indemnity purporting to be genuinely made by the late tycoon to enable them to secure a bank loan of Sh1.5 billion from Kenya Commercial Bank.

Mwangi and Aweys also face another charge of stealing valuation reports, certificate of a lease, PIN certificate and feasibility study booklet belonging to the deceased.

The hearing of the case, which has also been stalling since 2017, is also expected to proceed.

The prosecution is expected to present a former national bank of Kenya official as a witness.

Eight witnesses have already testified, and 14 are yet to do so.
