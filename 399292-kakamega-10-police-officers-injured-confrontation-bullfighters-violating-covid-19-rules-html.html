Kakamega: 10 police officers injured in confrontation with bullfighters for violating COVID-19 rules

Some 10 police officers are nursing injuries after clashing with a crowd of bullfighters at the Malinya stadium in Ikolomani, Kakamega County.

According to a police report seen by TUKO.co.ke, the contingent of 60 officers had gone to disperse the bullfighters when over 500 members of the public descended on them without any provocation.

The report mentions Geoffrey Matioli and Petro Njomu as being the main inciters.

Apart from the police officers, four police Land Cruisers were damaged by stoned thrown by the rowdy crowd.

The injured officers were treated at the Shibwe level four sub-county hospital and allowed to go home.

As at the time of going to press, no arrests had been made.

Bullfighting is a cultural sport that has continued to attract mammoth crowds in the county despite the national government’s bid to enact an animal protection law that will regulate it.

This was not the first time police officers and residents of Malinya were at loggerheads over the sport.

On October 17, 2020, there was another confrontation when cops tried to stop the crowd from holding a ceremony in which Sanchez, the new king of bullfighting, was to be crowned.

Sanchez was to become the reigning king after his predecessor, Malinya Pogba, died the same morning. Pogba was a bull owned by politician Bonny Khalwale.

As the crowd continued to swell, social distancing laws were flouted, prompting the officers to swing into action.

The running battles lasted two hours as teargas and gunshots rented the air, after which the crowd gathered again and continued with their mission.

When questioned over the persistence to hold the events despite perennial problems with law enforcement officers, the chairman of bullfighting Hudson Amalemba explained that the fighting helps tone down aggression in the animals

"The bulls are now attacking people in homes,” he said.

Bullfighting is held in high regard among the people of Kakamega, so much that the county government plans to renovate the Khayega grounds into a proper bullfighting stadium.

It is hoped that this will help promote the sport, as well as train the locals on how to turn it into a money-making venture that will alleviate poverty.

The incidence continues to cast spotlight on the seemingly growing animosity between civilians and law enforcement agencies as citizens continue to express dissatisfaction over enforcement of COVID-19 protocols.

This points to the growing need for sensitization on both parties that will promote respect and coexistence between civilians and the police service.

That said, the National Police Service Act of 2011 Section 103 (a) prohibits civilians from willfully resisting arrest, obstructing or assaulting law enforcement agencies during execution of their duties.
